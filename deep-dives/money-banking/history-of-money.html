The History of Money

From shells to Bitcoin: a 10,000-year journey through humanity's most important technology. Each era reveals what money needs — and why it keeps failing.

Section 1 of 6 · The pattern
Starting the investigation: who gets to create money, who receives it first, and what happens when that power is discretionary? We begin where the pattern begins.
🤝
The Barter Problem
~10,000 BCE — Before Money
▼

Before money, people traded directly: your wheat for my fish. This works in tiny communities but breaks down quickly. You need a "coincidence of wants" — both people must want what the other has, at the same time, in the right quantities.

No store of value Not divisible Requires coincidence of wants
KEY LESSON

Humans need a medium of exchange — something everyone agrees has value, so you don't need to find the one person who wants exactly what you have.

🐚
Commodity Money: Shells, Beads & Salt
~3,000 BCE — Natural Money
▼

Societies converged on scarce natural items as money: cowrie shells in Africa and Asia, wampum beads in North America, salt in the Mediterranean (the word "salary" comes from salt). These worked because they were portable, somewhat scarce, and widely recognized.

Widely accepted Somewhat portable Not truly scarce Fragile
WHY IT FAILED

When Europeans arrived in Africa with ships full of cowrie shells, they could "print money" by importing what locals used as currency. The shells were scarce locally but not globally. Any money that can be easily produced will eventually be devalued.

⬇
🪙
Metal Coins: Gold & Silver
~600 BCE — Hard Money
▼

The Kingdom of Lydia (modern Turkey) minted the first standardized coins around 600 BCE. Gold and silver emerged as the best monetary metals: scarce, durable, divisible, and beautiful. For 2,500+ years, gold and silver coins were the global standard.

Scarce (hard to mine) Durable (doesn't rot) Divisible Universally valued Heavy to transport Can be debased by rulers
THE DEBASEMENT TRAP

Every empire that used gold coins eventually debased them — mixing in cheaper metals to fund wars and spending. Rome, Byzantium, Spain, England — the pattern is universal. If rulers can debase money, they will. This is why physical possession isn't enough; you need incorruptible scarcity.

⬇
📜
Paper Money: Promises on Paper
~1000 CE — Representative Money
▼

China invented paper money during the Song Dynasty. Instead of carrying heavy coins, merchants carried notes that promised redemption for gold or silver. This was more convenient — but introduced a critical vulnerability: the promise could be broken.

Lightweight & portable Convenient Requires trust in issuer Can be printed without backing
THE PRINTING TRAP

China's paper money experiment ended in hyperinflation. The Yuan Dynasty printed so much paper money that it became worthless. This same story repeated in France (John Law's Mississippi Scheme, 1720), the American colonies (Continental currency), and dozens of other examples. Unbacked paper money has repeatedly ended in overprinting and devaluation.

⬇
🏛️
The Gold Standard
1821–1971 — Constrained Paper
▼

The classical gold standard (1821–1914) had one of the more stable long-run price levels in modern history — but "stable on average across a century" is not the same as "calm." It still saw banking panics and sharp deflations. Paper notes were redeemable for gold at a fixed rate, which constrained governments: they couldn't issue much more money than they had gold to back.

Constrained supply International standard Low inflation Requires trust in governments Gold is centralized in vaults
HOW IT ENDED

WWI forced governments to abandon convertibility to fund the war. Bretton Woods (1944) created a partial standard: only the US dollar was convertible to gold. In 1971, Nixon "temporarily" ended gold convertibility — it's still "temporary" 50+ years later. Any money backed by a political promise will be broken when it becomes inconvenient.

⬇
💵
Pure Fiat: Money by Decree
1971–Present — Unlimited Paper
▼

Since 1971, all major currencies are pure fiat — backed by government decree and legal-tender laws rather than a hard asset. Measured by consumer prices, the dollar has lost roughly 87% of its purchasing power since 1971, and about 96–97% since 1913 (BLS CPI, 2026 — two different start dates, two separate claims). Global debt reached a record ~$348 trillion at the end of 2025, about 308% of world GDP (IIF Global Debt Monitor, February 2026).

Convenient (digital) Government backing Unlimited supply Requires trust Controlled by central banks Constant devaluation
THE CURRENT CRISIS

Global debt is now about 308% of world GDP at end-2025 (IIF Global Debt Monitor, February 2026), and major central banks' balance sheets expanded by trillions of dollars after 2008. Interest payments are taking up a growing share of government budgets. The pure-fiat era is just over 50 years old — historically short for a monetary system, and increasingly debated. Whether it is sustainable is one of the open questions this history is meant to help you weigh.

Bitcoin: Sound Digital Money
2009–Future — Rules-Based Money
▼

On January 3, 2009, the Bitcoin genesis block was mined with the message: "The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks." Bitcoin was born from the failures of every previous monetary system.

Fixed supply (21M) Decentralized Permissionless Censorship-resistant Borderless Self-custodial Transparent Programmable

The claim Bitcoin makes

Each earlier era taught a hard lesson. Bitcoin's design is an explicit response to each one — a set of claims to test, not yet a verdict:

  • Barter — money must be widely accepted → Bitcoin aims to be accepted globally
  • Shells — money must be genuinely scarce → a fixed 21 million cap
  • Coins — rulers debase money → no ruler can change Bitcoin's supply
  • Paper — promises get broken → rules enforced by math, not a promise
  • Gold standard — centralized backing fails → a decentralized network instead
  • Fiat — unlimited supply erodes value → a supply fixed in advance

Whether these claims actually hold — and what they cost — is the work of section 6. For now, note only that Bitcoin is the first entry in this history that tries to answer the pattern rather than repeat it.

The Pattern of Monetary History

Every form of money in history has been corrupted, debased, or abandoned. The pattern is always the same:

Sound money → Trust accumulates → Power centralizes → Trust is abused → Money fails → Cycle repeats

Bitcoin's design is an attempt to break this cycle by minimizing how much you have to trust any single operator. Its rules are enforced by a decentralized network rather than a central authority, and its supply schedule is fixed and public — so there is no issuer who can quietly expand the supply. For the first time in this history, the monetary policy is set by code that anyone can audit, not by people you have to trust. Whether that holds up over time is exactly the kind of claim worth verifying for yourself — we put it to the test in section 6.

Why each step made sense at the time

It's tempting to read this history as a list of mistakes — as if people kept falling for bad money. They didn't. Every step was adopted because it solved a real, painful problem:

  • Paper over coins — metal was heavy and dangerous to carry; notes were portable and easier to protect.
  • Banks over mattresses — storing your own gold invited theft and loss; a vault offered safety.
  • Credit over cash-on-hand — trade outgrew the metal available; lending let real commerce scale.
  • Custodians and clearinghouses — settling across distances was slow and risky; shared ledgers made it fast.

Each was a genuine upgrade in portability, safety, convenience, credit, or scale. The uncomfortable pattern isn't that people were naive. It's subtler: every convenience handed someone a new point of control over the money — a vault that could lend out more than it held, an issuer who could print one more note, a government that could suspend the rule "just this once." Adoption was rational. The control came bundled with it.

Common misconception

"Each of these systems failed because the money was bad — or because people were too gullible to see it coming."

Mostly the opposite. Each new form worked — often for generations — precisely because it solved something real. What failed wasn't the convenience; it was the concentration that came with it. Once a single party could expand the supply or break the promise, the temptation eventually won. So the lesson to carry forward isn't "avoid convenience" — it's to watch where the convenience puts the control. That's the thread the rest of this deep dive follows.
