The bond market is doing what bond markets do when they stop pretending.

Long-dated sovereign debt is selling off. Buyers are demanding higher yields to hold the same paper. Mortgage and consumer borrowing costs are rising in turn.

None of this proves Bitcoin wins.

It explains why the Bitcoin question keeps coming back.

For advisors, families, and anyone watching local banking rails get slower and more political, the interesting question right now is not whether Bitcoin replaces Visa at the grocery store. It is something quieter.

When the asset everyone calls *safe* needs more yield just to be trusted, who controls the exit?

That is the part of the Bitcoin conversation most pieces miss. The medium-of-exchange debate is the visible layer. The function underneath it is the one that matters: the ability to hold, move, borrow against, and step away from a unit of account without asking permission.

Capital controls are not a payment problem. Withdrawal limits are not a payment problem. Frozen accounts are not a payment problem. They are exit problems.

Bitcoin's deepest claim is not on speed. It is on exit.

That reshapes the whole *"is Bitcoin becoming money?"* debate. It also makes the answer layered: Lightning, Fedimint, Liquid, and Bitcoin-backed credit each address exit differently, with different trust profiles a careful reader can verify for themselves.
