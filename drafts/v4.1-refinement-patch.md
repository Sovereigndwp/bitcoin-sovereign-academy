## Why this patch exists

Two post-publication developments surfaced after v4 shipped (May 19, 2026):

1. **DMND × RootstockLabs Stratum V2 merge-mining partnership** (Bitcoin Magazine, May 2026) — DMND's Stratum V2 pool now enables miners to individually select RSK merge-mined block commitments, with rBTC sidechain rewards paid directly to miner addresses without pool revenue-sharing. This is the first production deployment of "miner sovereignty over transaction selection" extended from main-chain templates to a merge-mined sidechain's templates. `[PRESS]`

2. **Paul Sztorc's August 2026 eCash announcement** (news.bitcoin.com, CoinDesk, others — late April / early May 2026) — Sztorc and LayerTwo Labs unveiled an **August 2026 Bitcoin hard fork chain split with a 1:1 BTC coin claim**. The new chain (eCash L1) is a near-copy of Bitcoin Core that activates BIP300/301 via CUSF on its own chain (not on Bitcoin mainnet), with at least seven merge-mined drivechains live at launch (Truthcoin, Coinshift, Bitassets, Bitnames, Photon, a Zcash-style privacy chain, and others). The proposal includes a controversial treatment of Satoshi-era coins that has prompted community pushback — Calle (Cashu developer) calls BIP300 drivechains "fundamentally flawed"; broader community framing is "theft." `[PRESS]`

The v4 canon as published (commit `56aa3f46`, May 19, 2026) materially mischaracterizes development #2: `thesis-v4.md` line 167 frames the August 2026 fork as "Drivechain-hosted sidechain fee markets" — language consistent with the original BIP300/301-on-Bitcoin soft-fork activation path Sztorc had been pursuing for a decade. The actual announcement is a *hardfork chain split* that abandons the mainnet activation path. v4 line 167 is factually misleading and needs correction, not just tightening.

Development #1 (DMND × Rootstock) is additive corroboration on the miner-sovereignty thread already present in v4 but does not appear in the L0 narrative or the Part II L3 taxonomy. v4.1 adds it.

## Item (b) — F9(c) and F16(a) denominator: explicitly include merge-mining fees

**Location:** `falsification-framework-v4.md` F9 (line 254) and F16 (line 397).

**Current F9(c).** "L1 fees under 15% of total miner revenue (12-mo MA)"

**Current F16(a).** "L1 fee revenue stays below 15% of total miner revenue (12-mo MA)"

**Ambiguity.** "Total miner revenue" is not defined operationally. As of May 2026 with v4 published, this implicitly means subsidy + L1 fees. Merge-mining fee revenue (rBTC from Rootstock, BMM from drivechains if any activate, sidechain fees more broadly) has historically been small enough to ignore. As DMND × Rootstock and similar partnerships scale, that assumption breaks.

**Proposed v4.1 measurement protocol (applies to F9(c) and F16(a)):**

> "Total miner revenue" is operationally defined as the sum, in BTC-equivalent at time of receipt, of: (i) the subsidy block reward, (ii) L1 transaction fees, and (iii) merge-mining fee revenue (rBTC from Rootstock, BMM payouts from any active drivechain, and analogous sidechain payouts received by Bitcoin miners as a direct consequence of their Bitcoin proof-of-work). Sidechain native-token payouts (e.g., L2 governance tokens) that flow to miners in pool-bonus form are excluded. Merge-mining payouts in non-BTC denominations are converted at the spot rate at receipt, then to USD at the same exchange rate used for the broader fee calculation. The numerator of the F9(c) and F16(a) ratios remains *L1 fees only*.

**Effect on the falsifier.** The denominator gains a positive quantity, so the L1-fee ratio shrinks. The 15% threshold is *more easily met*, so F9(c) and F16(a) are *more likely to fire*. Tightening confirmed.

**Measurement source addition.** Add to F9 and F16 source lists: *Rootstock block explorer (rsk.co), Bitlayer / DMND merge-mining payout disclosures, and any future drivechain payout dashboards.* `[DATA]` `[DEV]`

**Why this matters.** Without this clarification, the F9 / F16 ratio quietly becomes ambiguous as soon as merge-mining payouts are non-trivial. A future reviewer could argue either inclusion (which fires F9/F16 earlier) or exclusion (which delays firing) — exactly the kind of ambiguity the methodology-immutability rule exists to prevent. v4.1 closes it on the *more strict* side, which is the only side allowed.

**Quarterly reporting addition.** Starting September 2026, the dashboard reports F9 / F16 ratios in two forms: subsidy + L1 fees only ("v4 legacy"), and subsidy + L1 fees + merge-mining ("v4.1 strict"). The v4.1 strict form is the official ratio for verdict purposes. The v4 legacy form is published alongside for one year to make the methodology change visible and reversible-if-needed at the September 2027 review.

## Item (d) — Rewrite of line 167 (the Sztorc paragraph): factual correction

**Location:** `thesis-v4.md` Part II, Layer 0 section, line 167. This is the single biggest edit in v4.1.

**Current text (v4, line 167):**

> **Drivechain eCash August 2026 fork** is best read as a structural response to the mining-economics crisis. Paul Sztorc's framing in the May 17, 2026 ecashblog.com post positions eCash as a security-budget solution via Drivechain-hosted sidechain fee markets, not just ideology `[PRESS]`. Whether the fork succeeds or fails, the demand for new miner fee sources is empirically demonstrated.

**Problem.** The phrase "Drivechain-hosted sidechain fee markets" is consistent with the original BIP300/301-on-Bitcoin soft-fork activation path Sztorc had been pursuing since 2017–2019. The actual late-April / May 2026 announcement is a **hardfork chain split**: a new L1 (near-copy of Bitcoin Core) that activates BIP300/301 via CUSF *on the new chain*, with 1:1 BTC→eCash coin claim at snapshot, ~7 merge-mined drivechains on day one, and contested treatment of Satoshi-era coins. The v4 wording implies Bitcoin mainnet gains drivechain functionality in August; this is incorrect.

**Proposed v4.1 text:**

> **Sztorc's August 2026 eCash hard fork** is the load-bearing recent development on the mining-economics axis, and one of the most contested social-layer events in Bitcoin's history. After roughly a decade pursuing BIP300/301 (Drivechains) as a soft-fork activation on Bitcoin mainnet — which never reached the activation threshold — Paul Sztorc and LayerTwo Labs announced in late April / early May 2026 that they will instead launch **eCash**, a hard-forked separate L1 chain (near-copy of Bitcoin Core) that activates BIP300/301 via CUSF on its own consensus, with a 1:1 BTC→eCash coin claim at the August 2026 snapshot. The eCash chain launches with seven merge-mined drivechain sidechains live on day one (Truthcoin for prediction markets, Coinshift as a DEX, Bitassets, Bitnames, Photon for quantum-resistant addresses, a Zcash-style privacy chain, and others). The proposal includes a contested mechanism for handling Satoshi-era coins (~1.1M BTC) that has drawn explicit pushback — Calle, the Cashu developer, calls BIP300 drivechains "fundamentally flawed" `[PRESS]`; broader community framing has used the word "theft" for the Satoshi-coin treatment `[PRESS]`. The fork creates real operational headaches for ETF custodians and corporate treasuries holding BTC at the snapshot (claim mechanics, tax treatment, jurisdictional fork-handling policy) — intersecting with v4's load-bearing ETF concentration risk. **What this means for the thesis.** First, the August 2026 event is *not* a Bitcoin mainnet protocol change; the v4 reading that "the demand for new miner fee sources is empirically demonstrated" still holds, but only as a statement about the eCash-chain ecosystem, not as evidence about Bitcoin's own post-halving fee market. Second, no F-criterion fires from the announcement itself — F17 (quantum) is unrelated, F1 (BTC dominance) depends on whether eCash accumulates meaningful market share post-launch, F8 (sovereign adoption) is unaffected unless a sovereign holder visibly mishandles the fork. Third, the contested fork-handling at ETF / treasury level is the realistic correlated-failure surface and is captured in new Part VII Scenario 5 below. The v3 framing of "Drivechain eCash" as a security-budget response was right in spirit and wrong in mechanism; v4.1 corrects this. `[PRESS]`

**Word count:** ~340 words. Replaces ~70 words in v4. Longer than the original because the development is materially bigger than v4 captured.

## Item (e) — New correlated-failure scenario: contentious hardfork chain split

**Location:** `thesis-v4.md` Part VII, after Scenario 4. Also mirror in `falsification-framework-v4.md` correlated-failure section (line 461 onward).

**Proposed new scenario:**

> **Scenario 5 — Contentious hardfork chain split with custodial-stack fork-handling failure.** A non-trivially-adopted Bitcoin hard fork (the August 2026 eCash event is the live example) creates two custody-stack failures simultaneously: (a) ETF custodians and corporate treasuries face fork-handling policy decisions under regulatory pressure; (b) the contested-fork-handling narrative damages the "Bitcoin's social contract is settled" claim that underwrites v4's monetary-anchor thesis. Fires F1 (narrative damage to BTC dominance — but only if eCash accumulates meaningful market cap), F8 (sovereign / institutional confidence — partial; depends on visible failures), F10 (regulatory response if the airdrop / Satoshi-coin treatment triggers enforcement). Estimated 2–3 falsifiers, with reputational risk exceeding direct fires.
>
> **The recoverability question.** Scenario 5 differs from Scenarios 1–4 in that the failure mode is social-coordination, not market or regulatory or technical. Recovery requires either (a) the fork failing to accumulate meaningful market share — historically the modal outcome of contested Bitcoin forks (BCH, BSV, BTG, BCHA all trended toward irrelevance) — or (b) the fork accumulating share but the BTC chain visibly maintaining its dominant security and adoption position. The framework treats Scenario 5 as a *watch list*, not an active alarm, pending the August 2026 outcome.

**Effect on verdict thresholds:** None. Scenario 5 is narrative addition only. The 0–4 / 5 / 6+ verdict structure is unchanged.
