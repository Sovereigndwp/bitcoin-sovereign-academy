We invented stronger locks, smarter math, and new forms of digital cash.

The real breakthrough wasn't faster payments.
It was removing the referee.

‚ùì What if Bitcoin had arrived 20 years earlier‚Äîwould governments have allowed it?

Context: In the 1990s, the U.S. government classified encryption as a weapon and tried to ban its export. PGP's creator faced legal threats. Bitcoin needed not just the technology, but a political climate where decentralized money could exist long enough to prove itself.

‚ùì Why is removing trust harder than removing friction?

Context: Pix, Bre-B, and FedNow solved friction (instant payments) by adding trust requirements (central authority sees everything). Bitcoin took longer because solving trust meant inventing an entirely new way for computers to agree‚Äîproof-of-work consensus.

‚ùì Which patch do you think changed history the most?

Context: Public-key cryptography gave us digital ownership. Time-stamping gave us a history that's hard to fake. Solving the double-spend problem in an open network made digital scarcity real. Bitcoin is where all three finally work together without a referee. Each patch was essential‚Äîbut none sufficient alone. Which breakthrough do you think was the hardest to achieve?

‚ùì Why did it take until 2009 to combine these pieces?

Hint: The technology existed. What was missing was the incentive design. Bitcoin's mining reward aligned selfish interests with network security. No one had figured out how to make honest behavior profitable at scale‚Äîuntil Satoshi.
