When Money Goes Wrong

You've seen what makes good money. Now, let's flip it.
What happens when money stops working—when rules shift, savings shrink, and control disappears?

Fiat currencies fail for three main reasons: inflation, centralization, and control.

Problem #1: Inflation — The Silent Tax

Since 1971, governments have been able to print money freely.
More money chasing the same goods means your dollars quietly lose value every year.

Problem #2: Centralization — When a Few Control Many

Behind every fiat currency is a small group of people with enormous power.
They decide how much money exists, who gets access, and how the rules change.

When they pull the wrong lever, entire economies shake.

🚨 Bank Failures & Bailouts

Your bank doesn't hold all your deposits—it lends them out.
When crises hit, withdrawals freeze or get "bailed out" with taxpayer money.

Recent Examples:

  • 2023: Silicon Valley Bank collapse ($209B in deposits at risk)
  • 2023: Credit Suisse forced merger after $5.5B loss
  • 2015: Greece bank holiday, ATM limits at €60/day

Problem #3: Control — When Money Isn't Yours

In the digital age, your money lives in databases you don't control.
A few keystrokes can freeze accounts or block payments—even if you did nothing wrong.

🚫 Real Cases:

  • 🇨🇦 2022: Protesters' accounts frozen
  • 🌍 2010: WikiLeaks banned from PayPal & Visa
  • 🇳🇬 2021: Nigeria froze activists' funds

💬 Socratic Question:

If someone else can stop your transaction, is it really your money?

When It All Goes Wrong

These aren't ancient history—they happened recently:

🇻🇪 Venezuela (2016–2019)

Hyperinflation over 10,000,000%. Bread bought with wheelbarrows of cash. Bitcoin use surged.

🇱🇧 Lebanon (2019–Today)

Withdrawals capped at $200/month. Savings trapped. Families ruined.

🇿🇼 Zimbabwe (2008)

$100 trillion bills became worthless. Entire life savings wiped out overnight.

🇩🇪 Weimar Germany (1920s)

Hyperinflation collapse. People carried money in wheelbarrows to buy bread.

🇬🇷 Greece (2015)

Bank closures and withdrawal limits. Capital controls imposed for months.

🇺🇸 What About the United States?

"Could that happen here?"
Many Americans assume the dollar is safe because it's the world's reserve currency.
But the numbers tell a different story.

U.S. national debt has grown from $5.6 trillion (2000) to over $37.7 trillion today, rising by nearly $3 billion every day.

This isn't wheelbarrow hyperinflation—but it's a slow-motion monetary crisis.

When a government owes more than its economy produces (130% debt-to-GDP), and interest payments exceed defense spending, stability fades fast.
