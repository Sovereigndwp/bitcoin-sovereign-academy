When Money Goes Wrong

You've seen what makes good money. Now, let's flip it.

What happens when money stops working ‚Äî when the rules change, savings shrink, and control slips away?

Fiat currencies fail for three main reasons: inflation, centralization, and control.

üí• Problem #1: Inflation ‚Äî The Silent Tax

Since 1971, governments have been able to create new money at will.
When more money chases the same goods and services, prices rise ‚Äî and your dollars quietly lose value year after year.

You don't see money leaving your account.
You just notice that everything costs a little more each time you buy it.

üí∏ Inflation vs. Bitcoin (2015 ‚Üí 2025)

Let's take the same $1,000 in two forms of money and see what happened over ten years.

üíµ Your $1,000 in 2015 (U.S. Dollars)

Then: $1,000 bought a decent laptop or a week-long trip.

Now: The same $1,000 buys a budget laptop or a weekend away.

üìâ Loss: ~25‚Äì30% purchasing power
Prices rose, your effort stayed the same ‚Äî but your money now buys less.

üí¨ Reflection:

‚Çø Your $1,000 in 2015 (Bitcoin)

By 2025, with Bitcoin around $70,000 per BTC,
your same 3 BTC are worth about $210,000.

üìà Purchasing Power: dramatically higher
Not because Bitcoin "grew," but because its supply never changed.

üí¨ Reflection:

Same $1,000. Same year.
One currency inflated its supply. The other kept its promise.

üìâ The Math of Inflation

In 2015, $10,000 in savings bought you a certain lifestyle.

By 2025, at ~3% inflation per year, you need about $13,400 to buy the same things.

You didn't spend. Your purchasing power shrank.

üß† Key Idea

Money should measure value, not lose it.
When supply can grow endlessly, value leaks away.
When supply is fixed, value is preserved.

Problem #2: Centralization ‚Äî When a Few Control Many

Behind every fiat currency is a small group of people with enormous power. They decide how much money exists, who gets access to it, and how the rules change.

When they pull the wrong lever, entire economies shake.

Central Bank Mismanagement

Too cheap money (low rates): Easy borrowing ‚Üí Prices surge

Too expensive money (high rates): Tight credit ‚Üí Recession risk
