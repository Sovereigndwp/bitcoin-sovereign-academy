A Solution to the Money Problem

You've learned what makes good money (Module 1) and seen the problems with traditional currencies (Module 2). Now, meet Bitcoin: a completely new form of money that solves these problems through technology.

Bitcoin isn't a better version of the old system ‚Äî it's a rebuild from the ground up.

üí∏ Inflation vs. Bitcoin (2015 ‚Üí 2025)

Let's take the same $1,000 in two forms of money and see what happened over ten years.

üíµ Your $1,000 in 2015 (U.S. Dollars)

Then: $1,000 bought a decent laptop or a week-long trip.

Now: The same $1,000 buys a budget laptop or a weekend away.

üìâ Loss: ~25‚Äì30% purchasing power
Prices rose, your effort stayed the same ‚Äî but your money now buys less.

üí¨ Reflection: You didn't lose money. The money lost you.

‚Çø Your $1,000 in 2015 (Bitcoin)

In 2015, Bitcoin traded at roughly $333 per BTC.

Your $1,000 could buy ~3 BTC.

By 2025, with Bitcoin around $70,000 per BTC,
your same 3 BTC are worth about $210,000.

üìà Purchasing Power: dramatically higher
Not because Bitcoin "grew," but because its supply never changed.
Three BTC in 2015 is still three BTC in 2025.

üí¨ Reflection: Same $1,000. Same year.
One currency inflated its supply. The other kept its promise.

üìâ The Math of Inflation

In 2015, $10,000 in savings bought you a certain lifestyle.

By 2025, at ~3% inflation per year, you need about $13,400 to buy the same things.

You didn't spend. Your purchasing power shrank.

üß† Key Idea

Money should measure value, not lose it.
When supply can grow endlessly, value leaks away.
When supply is fixed, value is preserved.

The Origin Story

October 31, 2008 ‚Äî In the midst of the global financial crisis, as banks collapsed and governments printed trillions in bailout money, an anonymous person (or group) named Satoshi Nakamoto published a 9-page paper titled "Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System."

"The root problem with conventional currency is all the trust that's required to make it work. The central bank must be trusted not to debase the currency, but the history of fiat currencies is full of breaches of that trust." ‚Äî Satoshi Nakamoto

January 3, 2009 ‚Äî Satoshi mined the first Bitcoin block (the "Genesis Block") and embedded a message in the code:

"The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks"

This wasn't just a timestamp‚Äîit was a statement. Bitcoin was born from the failure of traditional financial systems.

2010-Present ‚Äî Satoshi disappeared in 2010, leaving Bitcoin to the world. No CEO. No company. No central authority. Just open-source code and a growing global community.

The idea spread worldwide ‚Äî not by marketing or mandates, but because it worked. Today, Bitcoin processes over $60 billion in transactions daily in nearly every country.

How Bitcoin Solves the Money Problems

You've seen the problems with traditional money in Module 2. Bitcoin addresses each one ‚Äî not through promises or policies, but through design. Let's explore how.

üíé Theme 1: Money You Can Trust

üíé

Absolute Scarcity

Bitcoin has a fixed supply of 21 million coins ‚Äî forever. This is enforced by mathematics and code, not promises. No one can print more Bitcoin, ever. When supply can't grow, value can't leak away.

üîç

Trust Through Verification

Every Bitcoin transaction is recorded on a public ledger visible to everyone. No secrets, no hidden inflation ‚Äî just math you can verify yourself. Don't trust. Verify.

üí™

Cheating Is Expensive

Changing Bitcoin's history would require more computing power than the rest of the world combined. The network rewards honesty and punishes fraud automatically ‚Äî cheating costs more than it's worth.

üí¨ Socratic Question:
If you can verify everything yourself, who do you need to trust?

üîì Theme 2: Money You Control

üîì

True Ownership

With Bitcoin, you control your money directly. No bank can freeze your account. No government can seize your funds without your private keys. No one can stop you from sending money anywhere, anytime.

üåê

No Central Authority

Bitcoin runs on 18,000+ independent computers (nodes) worldwide. No CEO, no company, no government control. To shut down Bitcoin, you'd need to shut down the entire internet ‚Äî globally.

‚ö°

Always On

Money shouldn't sleep when life doesn't. Bitcoin runs 24/7/365 ‚Äî no bank hours, no weekends, no holidays. Send money at 3 a.m. on Christmas if you need to. No permission required.

üí¨ Socratic Question:
What happens when no one has the power to stop you?

üåç Theme 3: Money for Everyone

üîí

Secure by Math, Not Politics

Bitcoin's rules are enforced by code and cryptography, not committees or promises. No one can rewrite the rules to "fix" an economic problem. Math is neutral ‚Äî it can't be bribed, lobbied, or voted out.

üë•

Open and Inclusive

Anyone, anywhere, can use Bitcoin with just a phone or internet connection. No credit score, no paperwork, no bank approval. One global network without discrimination ‚Äî 1.7 billion unbanked people suddenly have access.

üí¨ Socratic Question:
If money works for everyone everywhere, what boundaries remain?

üí° The Big Picture

Bitcoin doesn't just fix money.
It rebuilds it around honesty, freedom, and access ‚Äî for everyone, everywhere, all the time.

No promises. No politics. Just math you can verify.
